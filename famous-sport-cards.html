Six of the Most Famous Sports Cards

These are famous because they combine legend-level players + scarcity + collector hype + historic sales. Values below are based on major reported sales (aka: not “some guy on eBay with a dream”).

1952 Topps Mickey Mantle #311

The “king” of post-war baseball cards. It’s iconic, it’s Topps’ early era, and high-grade copies are insanely rare. This card is basically the hobby’s blue-chip stock.

Record reported sale: $12.6 million (Aug 2022, Heritage Auctions; SGC 9.5).

1909–1911 T206 Honus Wagner (tobacco card)

The original “holy grail.” It’s famous for extreme rarity and the story around production being stopped early. Whether every detail of the legend is perfect or not, the result is the same: Wagner is THE scarcity icon.

Major reported sale: $7.25 million (Aug 2022, private sale).
Recent auction example: $5.124 million (Goldin, Feb 2026).

1914 Baltimore News Babe Ruth (Rookie / early card)

An ultra-rare early Ruth card from his Baltimore Orioles (minor league) days. It’s a “history of baseball” type card, not just a collectible.

Major reported sale: $7.2 million (Dec 2023).

1986–87 Fleer Michael Jordan Rookie #57

The basketball rookie card. Even people who don’t collect know it. Condition matters a ton — centered, clean edges, and that classic Fleer look.

Record reported sale (PSA 10): $840,000 (Jul 2021).

2000 Playoff Contenders Tom Brady Rookie Autograph

The modern football grail. Autograph + scarcity + Brady being Brady. This is the card that made “modern” feel like vintage money.

Record reported sale: $3.107 million (Jun 2021, Lelands; BGS 9).

1979 O-Pee-Chee Wayne Gretzky Rookie #18

The ultimate hockey rookie card. The O-Pee-Chee version is notoriously tough in high grade because of print quality. Top grade copies are basically unicorns.

Record reported sale: $3.75 million (May 2021, private sale via Heritage).

Why these cards stay on top

The formula is pretty consistent: legendary player + iconic set + low supply (especially in high grade) + big public sales that turn into hobby folklore.
